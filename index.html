About Me

I am a college English instructor turned full stack software engineer. I enjoy weaving my academic interests together with my new-found love of code, with a particular focus on the ways in which coding and technology can help make everyday life a little bit easier and a little bit better for everybody.

I began my journey to code in late in 2017. I fumbled around on websites like W3schools and MDN and spent a few bucks for online courses. These were ok, but they lacked the kind of deep and immersive educational experience that I had become accustomed to over the previous ten years of my academic life. It was enough, however, to get me thinking seriously about a career change. I spent a couple months researching bootcamps as well as more traditional (academic) routes, narrowing my choices down before finally deciding on Fullstack Academy of Code. I began my bootcamp career with the Remote Bootcamp Prep in a Month course followed by the 24 week Flex Part-Time Immersive in New York City. It was simultaneously the most rewarding, most challenging, and most frustrating endeavor I had undertaken in my life.

Creativity

As an academic in the humanities, and especially from an English Department with a rich history of philosophical discourse and post-structural attitudes, I consider my approach to any challenge or problem to be heavily influenced by rigor and logic. However, within any formalized structure, it is creativity -- the ability to seek out new and meaningful connections within a framework -- that is of the greatest import.

There is a famous, somewhat misattributed quotation the makes the rounds on nearly every blog and in many an essay or book on writing. The quotation, which ostensibly comes from T.S. Eliot (and perhaps by way of a Joseph Heller interview), goes something like this:

When forced to work within a strict framework the imagination is taxed to its utmost and will produce its richest ideas. Given total freedom the work is likely to sprawl.
I believe that there is something quite appropriate here in the marriage of this literary / poetic maxim and the act of writing code. One of the most interesting and challenging aspects of writing code is the need to operate within a framework -- but how one operates within that framework is where the real magic happens.
